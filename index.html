Art Portfolio

I make art to make sense of the world around me — the people I notice, the places I love, and the cultural noise I'm constantly swimming through. My work moves between observation and abstraction, using whatever medium fits the idea: color pencil for the intimacy of a portrait, watercolor for the looseness of a landscape, oil for something I want to sit with longer. I'm drawn to quiet tension — a figure turning away, a familiar building under an impossible sky — where the ordinary tips into something slightly unreal.

Selected Works

Evanesce Away

Pastels, oil pastels, color pencils · 2024

The word "evanesce" means to gradually fade or vanish — and this piece began with that feeling, the sense of watching someone slip just out of reach. The initial image came from a scene in Mission Impossible where Tom Cruise steps into what looks like an ordinary photo booth and is instantly transported somewhere else entirely. That idea — a mundane enclosure becoming a threshold, a place where you disappear — stayed with me and became the foundation for this piece. The subject stands near a payphone booth, her hand raised and gaze turned away from the viewer, as if she's already somewhere else. I was interested in the contrast between her physical presence and her emotional absence, the way a person can be fully visible and still feel like they're disappearing. The background evolved from plain white to an abstracted field of light — purples, pinks, gold, and blue — that I built up in layers, trying to make it feel like an interior world bleeding out around her. Working in pastels and color pencil meant I could blend and layer gradually, pushing the saturation in the background while keeping the figure's skin tones grounded and warm. What I kept returning to was the tension between the hyper-rendered and the dissolving — how much detail it took to make something feel like it was fading.

Social Media

Watercolor, color pencils · 2025

This piece started as a question: what does it feel like to exist inside the constant judgment of social media? I wanted to make that experience visible and a little absurd, which led me to the image of a girl blowing a bubble — casual, indifferent, maybe bored — while everything around her represents the structures of online performance. The face mannequins on the wall behind her came early in the concept; they're blank and featureless, functioning like a silent audience watching without reacting. The reaction bar along the bottom is borrowed directly from a messaging interface, reducing the complexity of human response to six symbols. She's wearing a school uniform and holding a tie, which was about conformity and the way institutions shape how we present ourselves even before social media gets involved. I built the piece up from pencil sketches of the face, then laid down a coffee wash for the warm golden background tones before adding watercolor and color pencil detail. The challenge was making a piece that felt critical without feeling heavy-handed — the bubble she's blowing does a lot of that work, keeping things light even as the image around her gets more loaded.

Hegeman Chapel

Acrylic paint, pastels · 2025

Hegeman Chapel is a place I know well, and I'd always wanted to paint it — but a straightforward architectural painting felt too static for what the building means to me. The idea for the aurora came later, after I saw a photograph of the northern lights that I couldn't stop thinking about, and I realized what the chapel study was missing was a sky with that kind of presence. Both references are real: the chapel photographed on a still night, the aurora captured somewhere else entirely. Combining them meant inventing a scene that never actually happened — which is where the painting got interesting. I started with a pencil outline of the architecture, then built the warm brick in watercolor, working outward to the trees and ground. The sky came last, and it changed everything; once the aurora went in, the whole piece shifted from a landscape to something closer to a memory or a wish. What the piece is really about, I think, is the way a familiar place can suddenly feel enormous.

Chicago Bean

Pen, watercolor · 2025

I visited Millennium Park on a trip to Chicago with my brother and spent a long time standing in front of the Bean — longer than I expected to. What struck me wasn't the sculpture itself but what it did to everything around it: the skyline bent and flattened, the crowd reflected and miniaturized, the whole city compressed into a curved silver surface. That experience felt worth drawing. I started in pen, which forced me to commit to every line — the dense crosshatching of the buildings, the precise curves of the Bean's surface, the loose scatter of people in the foreground. Pen doesn't forgive mistakes, and working in it made me slow down and look more carefully than I usually do. The watercolor came after, and I kept it deliberately loose so it wouldn't compete with the line work — the blue of the sky, the warm autumn orange of the trees, the sandy color of the plaza. The Bean's reflection in the final piece is mostly suggestion rather than accuracy, which felt right; the point was never photographic precision but the feeling of standing there and watching a city become a distorted version of itself.

Fairway

Oil pastels · 2025

This painting came out of a family trip to a golf course — one of those rare days when my whole family was actually together. My brother is in his twenties and we live in different parts of the country; my parents are Korean and the four of us being in the same place at the same time happens maybe once a year, sometimes less. We played a round together, and what I kept noticing wasn't the game itself but the landscape — the way the fairway rolled toward the water, the hills sitting heavy and green in the background, the quality of light on everything. I wanted to paint that specific afternoon, that specific feeling of being somewhere unhurried with people you don't get enough time with. The two figures — one mid-swing, one watching — are small against the landscape, which was intentional; I wanted the course to be the subject, the people just evidence that someone was there. Oil pastels push color in a way that felt right for this — the greens ended up more saturated than anything I saw in real life, but they feel true to how the day registered emotionally. I started with loose color blocking, leaving the figures as white outlines until the landscape was nearly finished, so they'd have to exist within the painting's logic rather than driving it. The emphasis stays where I wanted it — on the sweep of the course, the light on the water, the sense of an afternoon that felt both ordinary and rare.
